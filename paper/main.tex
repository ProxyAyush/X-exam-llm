\begin{abstract}
  The prevalence of hallucinations in large language models (LLMs) poses significant risks in high-stakes domains such as medicine. This paper introduces X-Exam, a formal multi-agent reasoning framework formulated as a minimax optimization problem. By partitioning the cognitive load across a Generator, a Cross-Examiner, and a Judge, X-Exam enforces adversarial scrutiny that significantly outperforms traditional cooperative self-correction. Our experiments across massive clinical benchmarks (MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA) and general reasoning tasks demonstrate statistically superior factual reliability and calibration. X-Exam achieved a substantial reduction in Expected Calibration Error (ECE), proving that adversarial dynamics yield more robust and reliable clinical assertions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in complex reasoning, yet their tendency to hallucinate remains a barrier to deployment in critical sectors. Traditional approaches to mitigating hallucinations, such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and Self-Refine, rely on cooperative refinement within a single model or a set of peer models. However, these systems often suffer from confirmation bias.

\section{The X-Exam Architecture}
We formalize the X-Exam framework as a minimax game:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{G} \max_{C} S(G(x), C(G(x)))
\end{equation}
where $G$ is the Generator, $C$ is the Cross-Examiner, and $S$ is the Judge's correctness score.
